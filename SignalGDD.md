# SIGNAL — Game Design Document

_Version 0.7 — Working Draft_

---

## 1. Overview

**Title:** Signal (working title)

**Genre:** Turn-based strategy / builder

**Platform:** Browser (TypeScript + Phaser.js or SvelteKit)

**Perspective:** Top-down tile map (Earth), node/network map (Space)

### Concept Summary

Signal is a turn-based strategy game in which the player manages a state-sponsored corporation representing a major geopolitical bloc — the EU, North America, China, or others. The game is set in a alternative history, starting in 1970 after the USA first lands on the moon and ending in approximately 2100. The story is driven by a **push factor** (climate change or geopolitical conflict) and a **pull factor** (a mysterious signal from the outer solar system). The player builds facilities, pursues projects, and — without directing research explicitly — unlocks technologies organically through their actions.

The game progresses across three eras: Earth, Near Space, and Deep Space. Earth slowly degrades as a productive base. The ultimate goal is to respond to the signal, though multiple victory conditions are available.

---

## 2. Setting & Tone

### Alt-history setting

Climate change is measurable and visible. Sea levels are rising. Certain regions are becoming economically marginal. Geopolitical blocs are in competition and occasional cooperation. The world still functions — society has not collapsed — but the pressure is real and growing.

### The Signal

An anomalous transmission has been detected from somewhere in the outer solar system — beyond Jupiter, perhaps from the direction of Saturn or further. Its origin is unknown. Its content is partially decoded — mathematical at first, then increasingly structured. It is not obviously threatening, but its urgency increases as the game progresses. It may be a beacon, a warning, a blueprint, or something else entirely. The player's research will gradually reveal its nature.

### Tone

Optimistic but serious. This is not a dystopia — humanity is capable of rising to meet the challenge. But the cost of inaction is real, and the player's choices have weight.

## 3. Core Resources

Three resources govern all player actions. They interact and create tension with each other throughout the game.

### 3.1 Funding

The economic lifeblood of the corporation. Pays for facility construction, project initiation, and maintenance costs.

- Generated by: economy facilities, completed commercial projects, Earth regional health
- Drained by: construction, project costs, facility upkeep, disaster recovery
- Risk: neglecting Earth conditions or the economy starves the player of Funding at critical moments

### 3.2 Materials

Physical resources required for construction and large-scale projects.

- Generated by: mining facilities on Earth, later asteroid belt operations
- Drained by: facility construction, major projects (launch facilities, space stations)
- Key arc: Earth mines deplete over time. The asteroid belt offers near-infinite Materials but requires significant investment to access. The player must transition their Materials base from Earth to space across the mid-game.

### 3.3 Political Will

Public and governmental support for the corporation's mission. Reflects the degree to which the player's bloc believes in and supports the programme.

- Generated by: project completions, signal events, breakthroughs, Earth welfare improvements
- Drained by: Earth neglect, project failures, time passing without visible progress, disasters
- Behaviour varies by bloc: democratic blocs have volatile Will (high ceiling, hard to maintain); authoritarian blocs have stable Will but lower ceiling and risk of catastrophic collapse if a major public failure occurs
- Will affects project costs (low Will = bureaucracy, cost overruns) and unlocks certain projects that require public mandate

## 4. Research System

### 4.1 Philosophy

The player never directly chooses what to research. Technology emerges as a consequence of what the player builds and does. This is the central design distinguisher of Signal.

### 4.2 Research Fields

Five fields accumulate points passively based on player actions:

| Field           | Primary Sources                                                 |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Physics         | Research stations, observatories, deep space arrays             |
| Mathematics     | Universities, computing centres, signal analysis projects       |
| Engineering     | Industrial complexes, launch facilities, manufacturing plants   |
| Biochemistry    | Medical centres, agricultural facilities, life support research |
| Computing       | Data centres, communications networks, AI research facilities   |
| Social sciences | Universities, media centres, political projects                 |

### 4.3 Technology Discovery

Each technology has a **recipe** — a required threshold of field points across multiple fields. For example:

- _Orbital Mechanics_ might require Physics 40 + Mathematics 30 + Engineering 20
- _Closed-Loop Life Support_ might require Biochemistry 50 + Engineering 35 + Computing 20

**Recipes are randomised per playthrough.** The same technology may demand a different field balance each game, preventing experienced players from executing a fixed optimal route. Variance is moderate — the general shape of each tech is stable, but exact proportions shift.

### 4.4 Discovery Stages

Technologies progress through three visibility stages:

1. **Unknown** — the technology does not appear in the interface at all
2. **Rumour** — a vague text hint appears in the research feed (e.g. _"Your engineers are discussing unconventional propulsion concepts"_)
3. **Progress** — the technology is named and a progress bar appears; the player can now see how close they are

### 4.5 Cross-Field Breakthroughs

Some technologies only emerge when two or more fields are _both_ at sufficient levels simultaneously. These cross-field discoveries reflect real scientific history — Navigation emerging from the intersection of Astronomy and Mathematics; Computing advancing due to cryptographic needs during wartime. Cross-field breakthroughs are often powerful and surprising, and reward players who build diverse institutions rather than hyper-specialising.

### 4.6 Signal-Derived Technologies

As the signal is decoded, it may yield technological insights that could not be derived from human research alone. These are rare, powerful, and tied to the wormhole victory path. They appear only for blocs that have invested in signal analysis infrastructure.

## 5. Map & Infrastructure

### 5.1 Earth Map (Era 1)

A tile-based map representing a geopolitical bloc's territory. Tiles have types (urban, industrial, coastal, highland, forested, etc.) that affect what can be built on them and at what cost.

**Climate Degradation:** Over time, coastal tiles flood and become unavailable or reduced in productivity. Resource tiles deplete as mines are exhausted. The player watches their map slowly shrink and weaken — this is intentional, gradual pressure rather than a sudden crisis.

**Adjacency Bonuses and Penalties:** Facilities interact with their neighbours.

- University + Research Lab → collaborative bonus (both gain field points)
- Industrial Complex + Launch Facility → supply chain bonus (reduced project costs)
- University + Military Installation → penalty (brain drain, secrecy culture)
- Coastal facilities risk flooding damage over time

### 5.2 Near Space Map (Era 2)

Unlocked by completing the _Orbital Station_ landmark project. A second map layer representing Low Earth Orbit, the Moon, and Lagrange points. Mechanically distinct from Earth — distance and launch costs govern adjacency rather than geography. Facilities here are expensive but not subject to climate degradation.

### 5.3 Deep Space / Asteroid Belt Map (Era 3)

Unlocked by completing the _Lunar Base_ or _Deep Space Transit_ landmark projects. A node-network map (not a tile grid). Prospecting reveals resource nodes; the player establishes mining operations and builds transit routes between them. The asteroid belt provides near-infinite Materials. This layer is where the endgame economic base lives.

Further nodes may unlock toward the Jovian moons and beyond, guided by the signal.

## 6. Facilities

Facilities are persistent infrastructure that generate resources and field points each turn. They are built on map tiles (or nodes in space) and have upkeep costs.

### Sample Facility Types

| Facility             | Fields Generated     | Resource Effect    | Notes                                                  |
| -------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| University Campus    | Mathematics, Physics | —                  | Adjacency bonus with research facilities               |
| Research Laboratory  | Physics, Computing   | —                  | Bonus when adjacent to University                      |
| Industrial Complex   | Engineering          | Materials (small)  | Adjacency bonus with Launch Facility                   |
| Mine / Quarry        | —                    | Materials          | Depletes over time                                     |
| Economic Zone        | —                    | Funding            | Required to maintain Funding flow                      |
| Medical Centre       | Biochemistry         | Will (small)       | Improves population welfare                            |
| Observatory          | Physics, Mathematics | —                  | Accelerates signal-related techs                       |
| Deep Space Array     | Physics, Computing   | —                  | Required for signal decoding; hints at signal progress |
| Launch Facility      | Engineering          | —                  | Required for space projects; expensive                 |
| Orbital Station      | All fields (small)   | Funding, Materials | Era 2 facility; cross-field bonus                      |
| Asteroid Mining Node | —                    | Materials (large)  | Era 3; replaces Earth mining                           |

## 7. Projects

Projects are finite goals with defined costs and one-time rewards. They are distinct from facilities — they are things the corporation _does_ rather than things it _builds_.

### Project Types

**Landmark Projects** gate era transitions. They are expensive, visible, and celebrated.

- _First Satellite_ → unlocks Near Space map layer access
- _Crewed Orbital Station_ → fully opens Era 2
- _Lunar Base Establishment_ → opens Era 3
- _First Asteroid Mining Operation_ → activates the belt node map

**Scientific Projects** generate large field point bonuses and may accelerate specific techs.

- _Cancer Cure Programme_ → large Biochemistry gain, major Will boost
- _Climate Modelling Initiative_ → Physics + Mathematics gain; reveals climate timeline more clearly
- _Signal Decoding Project_ → requires Deep Space Array; advances signal understanding

**Commercial Projects** generate Funding and occasionally Will.

- _Global Communications Satellite Network_ → recurring Funding income
- _Space Tourism Programme_ → Will boost, Funding, mild political risk

**Narrative Projects** are event-driven and appear based on game state. They represent responses to world events and often have branching outcomes.

## 8. Other Blocs

### 8.1 Design Philosophy

Other blocs are **simulated presences**, not full AI competitors. They exist to make the world feel inhabited and create geopolitical texture, not to play the game in the same way as the player. They behave like weather systems — they have state, they evolve, they affect the player — but the player is not _playing against_ them in a traditional sense.

### 8.2 Starting Blocs (Era 1)

Five or six blocs begin the game with distinct profiles and personalities:

| Bloc                      | Economic Strength | Material Wealth | Will Profile           | Victory Bias           |
| ------------------------- | ----------------- | --------------- | ---------------------- | ---------------------- |
| North America             | High              | Medium          | Volatile (democratic)  | Wormhole / Signal      |
| European Union            | Medium            | Low             | Volatile (democratic)  | Ecological Restoration |
| East Asian Cooperative    | High              | Medium          | Stable (authoritarian) | Terraforming           |
| African Union             | Low               | High            | Medium                 | Economic Hegemony      |
| Petro-State Coalition     | High (early)      | High (early)    | Declining              | Obstruction            |
| Southern Pacific Alliance | Low               | Low             | High                   | Scientific wildcard    |

The **Petro-State Coalition** is a special case: their economic model is directly threatened by the game's push factors. They are a source of negative events — political interference, funding pressure, occasional sabotage — without being a military actor. They decline across the game.

### 8.3 Bloc Simulation

Each turn, blocs advance their stats using simple weighted rules. They make "decisions" based on their victory bias and current resource levels. The player never sees the simulation directly — they see its _outputs_: completed projects announced in a news ticker, new facilities appearing on a world map view, and diplomatic events.

### 8.4 Bloc Decline and Elimination

As climate change progresses and resources deplete, some blocs lose relevance. Elimination is a narrative moment — a specific event fires:

> _"The Sahelian Federation has formally dissolved following a cascade of climate and economic shocks. Their deep space array, the most sensitive on Earth, now stands unmaintained."_

The player may then face a decision: invest to inherit and reactivate the asset, or let it go.

Blocs that survive into Era 2 may have changed character significantly from their Era 1 selves. A bloc that pivoted hard to space may have effectively abandoned Earth. An authoritarian bloc might fracture under the psychological stress of long-duration space operations.

By Era 3, only two or three blocs retain genuine off-world presence.

### 8.5 Diplomatic Events

Interaction with other blocs is event-driven rather than a full diplomacy system. Periodically, a card-style event fires:

> _"The East Asian Cooperative has completed their first orbital station. They are offering a materials exchange agreement."_
> → **Accept** (Materials boost; they gain research points) | **Decline** (no effect; small Will penalty)

These events write the story of the world without requiring full simulation.

## 9. Pressure Mechanics

### 9.1 Push Factor: Climate Change

The primary Earth degradation system. Each era, climate pressure increases. Coastal tiles flood on a probabilistic schedule (visible to the player in advance). Resource tiles deplete. Some regions become economically marginal.

- The player sees the degradation timeline — it is not a surprise
- Specific events fire at thresholds (e.g. _"Sea levels have reached the point where coastal industrial zones require flood defences or relocation"_)
- Earth never becomes uninhabitable in normal play — but it becomes progressively less productive
- Neglecting Earth entirely accelerates climate effects and drains Political Will

### 9.2 Push Factor: Geopolitical Tension (Optional Starting Condition)

If the player selects War as their push factor at game start, geopolitical tension is an additional pressure. Certain blocs become adversarial. Military events threaten facilities. Will is more volatile. This path gives the player a head start on propulsion-related technologies (military rocketry heritage) but at the cost of international cooperation options.

### 9.3 Pull Factor: The Signal

The signal is always present but changes in character across the game:

- **Era 1:** Faint, mathematical. Detected only by specialised facilities. Generates curiosity and a small Will bonus.
- **Era 2:** Structured and clearly artificial. Widely known. Generates significant Will. Competing interpretations emerge — is it an invitation, a warning, or something else?
- **Era 3:** Urgent. The signal's content is partially decoded. Its origin point is identified. The choice of how to respond defines the endgame.

The signal also introduces a **hostile interpretation loss condition** — not alien war, but the possibility that the signal is a warning about an imminent natural phenomenon (gravitational anomaly, stellar event) that the player has misread as an invitation. Civilisations that under-invest in signal analysis may fail to decode the warning in time.

## 10. Victory Conditions

### 10.1 Wormhole / Signal Response _(Primary / Canonical Victory)_

Decode the signal fully, develop the technologies it implies (potentially including reverse-engineered insights), and launch a mission to its origin point. This is the "true ending" — the one the game is secretly about. Requires deep investment in Physics, Mathematics, and Computing, plus signal analysis infrastructure.

_Thematic note:_ This victory path is lonely and requires the player to let Earth go. It is the hardest to explain to a sceptical public (Will management is crucial) and the most demanding scientifically. It rewards the player who committed earliest and most completely to the signal.

### 10.2 Ecological Restoration

Fight the climate pressure mechanic rather than routing around it. Restore Earth's biosphere, stabilise sea levels, and establish a sustainable civilisation that does not need to leave. Requires heavy Biochemistry and Engineering investment and sustained Political Will over a long game. Mutually exclusive in _feel_ if not in mechanics with the wormhole victory — one is hope, the other is redemption.

_Thematic note:_ This is the hardest victory because it requires the player to resist the pull of space when the signal is calling. It is the most emotionally resonant for players who feel the real-world weight of the climate theme.

### 10.3 Economic Hegemony

Achieve a state in which your bloc's infrastructure, standards, and systems become the foundation on which all other surviving blocs operate. Not elimination — indispensability. Other blocs still exist; they simply function within your framework. Requires sustained Funding dominance and high Political Will.

_Thematic note:_ This is the most accessible victory for new players and the least demanding scientifically. It risks feeling anticlimactic without strong narrative events to punctuate the economic arc.

### 10.4 Terraforming Mars

Complete a long multi-stage project to make Mars habitable. Stages: Atmosphere Generation → Water Unlocking → Biosphere Seeding. Each stage is a landmark project. This is the most visible and physical victory — the player watches a planet change. Requires the deepest Materials investment.

_Thematic note:_ This is the "head down, build things" path for players who love infrastructure optimisation. It is least connected to the signal, giving it a parallel-track quality — the player who pursues this may finish the game never fully understanding what the signal was.

## 11. Loss Conditions

### 11.1 Climate Collapse

The climate degradation system reaches a point of no return. Society contracts to a pre-industrial state. The corporation loses its mandate. This loss should never feel sudden — the player watches it approach across many turns, facing increasingly costly choices to delay it. The best version of this loss feels _deserved_.

### 11.2 Signal Misinterpretation

The signal was a warning, not an invitation. A player who under-invested in signal analysis, or who ignored it entirely, may face a late-game event they cannot adequately respond to — a rogue body on a collision course, a solar event, or a gravitational anomaly. This loss is preventable with adequate investment and is more likely when pursuing non-wormhole victory paths without maintaining minimal signal awareness.

### 11.3 Political Collapse

Political Will drops to zero and cannot recover. The corporation is dissolved, its mandate revoked by a public that has lost faith. This loss is most likely for democratic blocs that suffer a major public failure (a crewed mission disaster, a widely covered economic scandal) at a moment of already low Will.

### 11.4 The Abandoned Earth _(Moral Loss)_

Not a hard game over, but a post-game reckoning. The player achieved a space-based victory (wormhole or Terraforming) but neglected Earth so completely that civilisation on the home planet collapsed behind them. They survived — but at terrible cost. The post-game summary names this outcome explicitly. It is morally ambiguous rather than purely negative, and it is historically plausible.

## 12. Starting Bloc Selection

At game start, the player selects:

1. **Their bloc** — from 3-6 options, each with a different starting profile of Funding, Materials, Will, and research field biases
2. **Push factor** — Climate Change or Geopolitical Tension (affects starting conditions and event pool)
3. **Pull factor** — always the Signal, but its initial strength and first decoded content varies slightly by bloc (some blocs detected it earlier, have more data)

### Bloc Differentiation

Beyond starting stats, blocs differ in:

- **Will mechanics** — democratic blocs: volatile, high ceiling; authoritarian blocs: stable, lower ceiling, catastrophic collapse risk
- **Starting tech biases** — some blocs begin with partial progress in certain fields (e.g. a scientifically-focused bloc starts with elevated Mathematics and Physics)
- **Event pool** — some events are bloc-specific (EU faces internal political fragmentation events; North America faces public opinion volatility; China faces succession/stability events)
- **Victory path costs** — some victories are cheaper for certain blocs (Terraforming is cheaper for the East Asian Cooperative; Ecological Restoration is cheaper for the EU)

## 13. Card System & Turn Structure

### 13.1 Overview

The player interacts with the game through a hybrid card system. Rather than a pure menu-driven action list or a fully randomised deck-builder, the system combines reliable standing actions with a drawn hand of opportunity cards. This creates turns that feel both legible and surprising.

### 13.2 The Three Card Zones

**Standing Actions** — always visible, always available (outside special events). Not drawn cards — a persistent toolbar representing routine organisational operations. Examples: Build Facility, Initiate Project, Adjust Budget, Standard Diplomacy. These do not deplete and are available every turn. They form the backbone of every turn; the player can always make progress even with a weak hand.

**Action Hand** — drawn each turn from the player's unlocked deck. Typically 4–5 cards. These are opportunities, special moves, and powerful one-offs. Examples range from _Academic Conference_ (Physics + Mathematics gain) in the early game to _Asteroid Prospecting Expedition_ and _Decode Signal Segment_ in the late game. The hand is the primary source of strategic variety and surprise.

**Event Zone** — a separate area where incoming world events land. Events sit with a visible countdown, demanding a response within N turns. Some are opportunities (accept or decline); some are crises that must be addressed or absorbed. Events are framed as cards in the same visual language as action cards, keeping the UI consistent.

### 13.3 The Banking Mechanic

The player may hold up to **2 cards** between turns in a bank. Banked cards do not expire automatically but carry a **decay cost** of 1 Funding per card per turn — representing the organisational overhead of keeping an initiative "warm" without acting on it.

If the player draws new cards when the bank is full, they must discard to make room. This creates deliberate, sometimes painful decisions about which opportunities to preserve.

Banking is primarily used to hold counter cards in reserve against anticipated events — see Section 13.4.

### 13.4 Counter Cards

Some action cards have a secondary **counter** function, allowing them to be played reactively against an event rather than proactively as an action. Counter use is always available during the Event phase and typically costs significantly more than the card's normal use.

| Card (Normal Use)    | Counter Use                         | Cost Multiplier                       |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Political Lobbying   | Neutralise: Government Interference | High Will cost                        |
| Emergency Budget     | Neutralise: Funding Crisis          | Sacrifices next turn's Funding income |
| Media Campaign       | Neutralise: Public Scandal          | High Funding cost                     |
| Security Protocol    | Neutralise: Sabotage Event          | Materials + Funding                   |
| International Summit | Neutralise: Bloc Conflict           | Will + banked card slot               |

The high cost is intentional. Countering an event should feel like a genuine sacrifice, not a free dodge. A player who keeps a Political Lobbying card banked as insurance against interference events is making a real strategic hedge — paying decay costs as a premium against future risk.

### 13.5 Event Response Tiers

Not every event has a full counter. Three response tiers exist:

1. **Full counter** — a specific card neutralises the event entirely, at significant cost
2. **Partial mitigation** — a standing action or resource payment reduces the event's impact (e.g. spend 20 Funding to halve the Will damage from a public scandal)
3. **No counter** — some events simply happen. Climate flooding a coastal tile; a rival bloc completing a major project; a key scientist retiring. These are absorbed and adapted to, not solved.

The third tier is important. The player should never feel that perfect play counters everything. Some events are the world doing what the world does.

### 13.6 Deck Evolution

The player's deck reflects the state of their civilisation. It grows and changes as technologies unlock and eras advance.

- **Era 1 deck** — economic, political, and basic science cards: Build Mine, Lobby Government, Academic Conference, Grant Application, Recruit Engineers
- **Mid-game additions** — as technologies unlock, new cards enter the deck automatically: Satellite Survey, Orbital Insertion, Signal Analysis Session, Climate Modelling Run
- **Late game** — the deck contains things unimaginable at game start: Asteroid Prospecting, Wormhole Traversal Test, Decode Signal Segment, Terraform Atmosphere Seeding
- **Obsolescence** — some early cards are replaced by upgraded versions as technology matures. Build Basic Mine becomes Automated Extraction. This makes progress feel tangible.

When a new card enters the deck due to a research breakthrough, a brief flavour note appears: _"Your materials scientists have refined deep-bore extraction. Automated Extraction is now available."_

### 13.7 Standing Action Restrictions

Rarely, special events may temporarily restrict standing actions — creating moments of genuine constraint. Examples:

- _Government Audit_ — Build action suspended for 2 turns unless countered
- _Supply Chain Collapse_ — Materials-based standing actions cost double this turn
- _Public Inquiry_ — Will-generating actions locked; Will drains until resolved

These should be rare and significant. Frequent restriction of standing actions makes the player feel helpless rather than challenged.

## 14. Game Loop Summary

Each turn proceeds through five phases:

1. **Event Phase** — new event card(s) land in the event zone; existing events tick down their countdown; the player decides whether to counter any active events using banked or hand cards
2. **Draw Phase** — player draws up to their hand limit from their unlocked deck
3. **Action Phase** — player plays action cards and/or uses standing actions in any order; turn ends when the player chooses or options are exhausted
4. **Bank Phase** — player may move up to 2 cards to their bank (paying decay cost next turn); remaining unplayed hand cards are discarded
5. **World Phase** — resources tick, research fields accumulate, climate progresses, other blocs advance, signal strengthens or sends a new event

Research is never a direct action. It accumulates _because of_ what the player built and the cards they played.

## 15. Tech Stack Recommendation

_(For reference when beginning implementation)_

- **Frontend:** TypeScript + Phaser.js (tile map rendering, scene management, asset handling)
- **Web framework (if needed for UI-heavy views):** SvelteKit — preferred over heavier frameworks for its minimal runtime, native TypeScript support, and clean component model; can be combined with Phaser for UI overlays (HUD, card hand, event zone) while Phaser handles the map canvas
- **Build tooling:** Vite (native to SvelteKit; also works standalone with Phaser)
- **Testing:** Vitest (game logic, research field calculations, event system)
- **Hosting:** AWS S3 + CloudFront (static assets; no server required for single-player)
- **Save system:** JSON snapshot to browser storage or file download — no account system required
- **Map authoring:** Tiled (exports to Phaser-compatible format; hex grid support built in)

## 16. Resolved Design Decisions

### 16.1 Turn Structure

**Decision: One year per turn, fixed calendar.**

Each turn represents one calendar year. This grounds the game in a human timescale, connects the climate degradation timeline to a countable turn track (sea level events, resource depletion milestones), and gives event flavour text a natural temporal framing.

The one-year cadence may be revised per era through playtesting. Era 1 (Earth) maps naturally to annual political and budget cycles. Era 2 and 3 may warrant a different rhythm — space operations and asteroid mining don't necessarily tick on the same cadence as election cycles. This should be treated as a design variable rather than a fixed commitment across the whole game.

**Implication for the card system:** banked cards decay at 1 Funding per year, giving the cost an in-world justification — keeping a programme initiative on the shelf for two or three years while waiting for the right moment carries real organisational overhead.

### 16.2 Bloc Identity Across Eras

**Decision: Blocs can merge, with a "go it alone" refusal option.**

As blocs decline and eras advance, the player's bloc may receive merger offers from weakening NPC blocs. This is handled as a time-limited event card, giving the player full visibility of what they would gain and lose before deciding.

**Voluntary mergers** arrive as offers with a 2–3 turn countdown. The player sees the incoming bloc's resource profile, field biases, Will characteristics, and liabilities before deciding. Accepting triggers a one-turn integration cost (Funding and Will penalty) before benefits materialise. The player's bloc identity may update to reflect the merger — a compound name, a shifted flag, a new bloc description.

**Forced mergers** occur when a collapsing bloc has nowhere else to go. These arrive as events rather than offers: _"The Sahelian Federation has collapsed. Significant refugee and industrial flows have merged into your territory."_ The player gains some assets and inherits some liabilities with no choice in the matter. This category of event is distinct from voluntary mergers and should feel different — heavier, more ambiguous.

**The go it alone option** is always available for voluntary mergers but carries a cost: a small Will penalty (the public wanted this; leadership refused), and the declining bloc becomes a passive client state at best, or an occasional source of negative events at worst if they had no other options. The go-it-alone path preserves strategic identity and keeps the player's resource profile clean — a legitimate and harder choice.

**Thematic examples:**

- EU absorbs a North African remnant bloc following climate displacement — gains Materials and Engineering field points, inherits political instability events
- North America merges with a collapsed Latin American federation — gains workforce and Biochemistry field bias, inherits debt liability
- East Asian Cooperative absorbs a Pacific island network — gains Computing expertise, loses nothing except Will (controversial domestically)

### 16.3 Multiplayer

**Decision: Single-player only.**

Multiplayer is explicitly out of scope. This is a positive design choice, not a deferral — the core experience of Signal (emergent research, narrative arc, the gradual letting-go of Earth) is not enhanced by multiplayer, and the architectural complexity it would introduce is not justified.

Single-player-only has meaningful benefits:

- Game state lives entirely client-side; no authoritative server needed
- Save/load is a simple JSON snapshot — no account system required
- The bloc simulation and event systems can be **deterministic from a seed**, enabling seeded runs. Players can share a seed string and compare how differently the same starting world played out — a lightweight community feature that costs almost nothing to implement and adds replayability texture.

### 16.4 Visual Style

**Decision: Abstracted hex grid.**

The Earth map uses a hex tile grid rather than a square grid or isometric view. Hex grids provide richer adjacency geometry (up to six neighbours per tile vs four for square grids), making facility placement decisions more strategically interesting. They also read immediately as "serious strategy" to experienced players, setting correct expectations.

**Aesthetic direction:** Clean and atmospheric rather than richly illustrated. Dark backgrounds with hex tiles that shift colour and saturation based on type and state — coastal tiles in deep blue-green, industrial tiles in warm amber, depleted or climate-damaged tiles desaturated and visually degraded. Functional and readable, with atmosphere carried by colour and state rather than detailed art assets. This is achievable for a small team and leaves room for the systems and narrative to carry the experience.

Phaser has solid hex grid support. Tiled (the map authoring tool) also supports hex grids natively and exports in a Phaser-compatible format.

The node/network map used for the asteroid belt (Era 3) is visually distinct — sparse, dark, with glowing nodes and transit lines — reinforcing that the player has left the terrestrial world behind.

### 16.5 Narrative Layer

**Decision: Named board characters as functional slots, minimal prose.**

The player's corporation is governed by a **Board** of 5–7 named characters filling defined role slots. Characters are functional — they provide buffs and debuffs — rather than fully characterised. No dialogue, backstory prose, or relationship systems are required at this stage.

**Board roles** (representative set, to be finalised):

- Chief Scientist
- Director of Engineering
- Head of Finance
- Political Liaison
- Director of Operations
- (Additional slots unlocked through progression)

Vacant seats carry a mild ongoing penalty — that function runs below capacity. Filling seats well is a quiet background priority that occasionally becomes urgent.

**Character traits** — each character has 1–2 buffs and typically one debuff. The debuff is essential; it makes characters trade-offs rather than free upgrades. Examples:

| Character          | Role              | Buff                          | Debuff                           |
| ------------------ | ----------------- | ----------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Dr. Yeva Marchetti | Chief Scientist   | +15% Physics field generation | −10% Funding per turn            |
| Col. James Okafor  | Security Director | Counters sabotage events free | −5 Will per turn                 |
| Min-Ji Seo         | Head of Finance   | +20% Funding income           | −5% Engineering field generation |

**Character acquisition** — characters surface through events and project completions. A research breakthrough may attract a renowned physicist; a completed medical project surfaces a biologist; a political crisis may force a resignation and open a seat. Characters can also arrive as action cards.

**Character lifecycle** — characters age, retire, resign, and occasionally die. Each character has a visible age or tenure indicator so the player can anticipate succession needs. Resignations can be player-triggered (sacrifice a board member to resolve a scandal event) or event-triggered. Death is rare but legitimate — losing a key character at a critical moment is a meaningful game event, not a surprise punishment.

**AI board members (Era 3)** — by the deep space era, AI characters become available to fill board seats. AI members carry stronger buffs with no human-style debuffs, but introduce a new event pool — questions of autonomy, unsettling unilateral decisions, and political controversy around non-human leadership. The first AI board member is a milestone unlocked by a specific research breakthrough, not a random event. A board that is majority AI by the wormhole endgame is a quietly transformed organisation — that transformation is part of the game's story without requiring explicit narrative.

**What is explicitly deferred:** portrait art, character dialogue, backstory text, inter-character relationship systems. These are optional additions for a later development phase.

### 16.6 The Signal — Endgame Specifics

**Decision: An ancient automated alien system, dormant until activated by humanity's first satellites.**

The signal is not a being. It has no personality, no agenda, no capacity for two-way communication. It is a _system_ — sophisticated, ancient, and indifferent — placed at the edge of the solar system hundreds of thousands of years ago by an intelligence that may no longer exist. It detected a threshold of technological development (the launch of humanity's first basic satellites) and activated automatically. Humanity did not discover the signal; humanity accidentally switched it on.

This framing is intentional and should be preserved across all event text and flavour writing. The signal is a lock, not a correspondent.

**Structure and content**

The signal contains two things:

1. **A warning** — the nature of which is decoded gradually through research investment. It relates to the wormhole (see below) and the consequences of an incorrect response.
2. **An opportunity** — a protocol. If humanity can decode the signal's full sequence and respond correctly, a wormhole or subspace portal at the heliopause is activated, providing access to wherever the system's builders intended it to lead.

The destination of the wormhole is never revealed within the game. It is the final mystery — the moment the credits roll and the player imagines the rest.

**The wormhole, not generic FTL**

The endpoint of the wormhole victory path is not invented faster-than-light technology. It is a _door_ — a fixed structure at the heliopause, built by something else, that humanity learns to open. This distinction matters:

- The wormhole is a place on the late-game map, a concrete objective the player's ships travel toward
- It cannot be replicated; there is only one door
- Studying the signal yields genuine Physics and Mathematics research field advances as a byproduct — the signal's encoding contains insights that push human science forward — but the wormhole itself is irreproducible technology

**The response mechanic**

Signal translation is a partially visible track separate from but connected to the research fields. Physics and Mathematics contribute to decoding progress, but the final translation is a distinct interpretive challenge, not a research threshold. Investment in signal analysis infrastructure (Deep Space Arrays, dedicated projects) narrows uncertainty and provides confidence indicators — but never certainty.

The climax of the wormhole path is a late-game event card presenting the player with a choice: _"Your linguists and physicists have prepared three candidate responses to the signal's final sequence. Choose one."_ Prior investment determines how many options are presented and how clearly their likely consequences are indicated. A well-prepared civilisation may reduce this to two options with strong confidence signals. An under-prepared one faces three options with no guidance.

**Consequences of an incorrect response**

- The wormhole option is permanently closed — the signal ceases the relevant transmission, the portal does not activate, and the wormhole victory path is no longer available in that run
- Ships that subsequently attempt to cross the heliopause are intercepted by automated defensive systems — not an alien war, simply a system doing what it was designed to do when an incorrect response is followed by an intrusion attempt
- This is a loss condition specific to the wormhole path; players pursuing other victories are unaffected but also cannot access the wormhole

**Consequences of a correct response**

- The wormhole activates at the heliopause — a new node appears on the deep space map
- A crewed mission through the wormhole is the final landmark project, and its completion triggers the wormhole victory
- The signal goes quiet after activation; it has done what it was built to do

**Note on revisability**

The specific nature of the signal's builders, the destination of the wormhole, and the exact content of the warning are deliberately left open for future narrative development. The mechanical and structural decisions above are stable; the lore details beneath them can be revised without breaking the game systems.
